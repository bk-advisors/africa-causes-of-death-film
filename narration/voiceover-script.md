# Voiceover recording script: 8.3 Million

One numbered line per audio clip. Save each take as `line-NN.wav`
(e.g. `line-01.wav`) into `apps/africa-causes-of-death-film/audio/`.

Recording notes:
- Quiet room, soft furnishings, phone on silent, fridge/AC off if audible.
- Mouth 6-8 inches from the mic, speaking slightly past it, not into it.
- Record 2 seconds of room silence at the start of the session (for noise reduction), and re-record any line you fluff as a fresh take of just that line.
- Read at a calm, even documentary pace. The film will stretch to fit your timing, so do not rush.
- The caption durations below are the current on-screen timings, purely as a pace reference.


## Chapter 1: The outbreak

**01** (`line-01.wav`, ~5s)
> Two months ago, another Ebola outbreak was declared in Uganda.

**02** (`line-02.wav`, ~5s)
> A confirmed case. A press conference. An emergency command centre. Global headlines.

**03** (`line-03.wav`, ~5s)
> It has happened before, and it will happen again.

**04** (`line-04.wav`, ~6s)
> Since 1976, Ebola has killed about 15,000 people: every outbreak, every affected country, combined.

**05** (`line-05.wav`, ~7s)
> That half-century total fits inside a single bad week of malaria on this continent.

**06** (`line-06.wav`, ~4s)
> That contrast got me thinking.

**07** (`line-07.wav`, ~7s)
> We organise our attention around emergencies. The causes that do most of the killing are almost never in the news.


## Chapter 2: 8.3 million

**08** (`line-08.wav`, ~7s)
> In 2021, 8.3 million people died in the WHO African Region, of everything, at every age.

**09** (`line-09.wav`, ~5s)
> Each dot on this screen is 1,000 of them. This is all of them at once.

**10** (`line-10.wav`, ~5s)
> Global health sorts these deaths into three big groups.

**11** (`line-11.wav`, ~8s)
> Communicable, maternal, perinatal and nutritional conditions, the diseases of poverty: 4.6 million deaths. More than half of everything.

**12** (`line-12.wav`, ~6s)
> Non-communicable diseases, meaning heart disease, cancer, diabetes, stroke: 2.9 million.

**13** (`line-13.wav`, ~6s)
> Injuries, from road crashes to drowning to violence: 770,000.

**14** (`line-14.wav`, ~7s)
> And one cause that fits nowhere neatly. The gold dots are COVID-19: 493,000 deaths here in 2021, its worst year on the continent.

**15** (`line-15.wav`, ~7s)
> But this three-way split hides the thing that matters most: when in a life each death lands.

**16** (`line-16.wav`, ~5s)
> Sort the same 8.3 million dots by age instead.

**17** (`line-17.wav`, ~7s)
> 2.7 million of them, one in every three, are children under five.


## Chapter 3: The first five years

**18** (`line-18.wav`, ~5s)
> Under five, the chart is a horror show.

**19** (`line-19.wav`, ~8s)
> Malaria: 450,000 children. The single largest killer of African children, from a disease we have known how to prevent for the better part of a century.

**20** (`line-20.wav`, ~6s)
> Lung infections, mostly pneumonia: 427,000. Most of them vaccine-preventable.

**21** (`line-21.wav`, ~9s)
> Preterm birth: 381,000 babies. Diarrhoeal disease, treatable with a thirty-cent packet of salts: 281,000. Birth asphyxia: 280,000.

**22** (`line-22.wav`, ~8s)
> Five causes, over 1.8 million child deaths in a single year. The cheapest, most proven interventions in public health line up against exactly this list.


## Chapter 4: The quiet years

**23** (`line-23.wav`, ~7s)
> If a child makes it past five, the next decade is statistically the safest stretch of a human life.

**24** (`line-24.wav`, ~6s)
> Deaths fall to about 463,000, less than a fifth of the under-five toll.

**25** (`line-25.wav`, ~7s)
> And the mix changes. After the lingering infections come the accidental hazards of childhood: road injuries and drowning, water, fire, traffic.

**26** (`line-26.wav`, ~6s)
> After malaria, the biggest single threat to a school-age African child is something no doctor and no vaccine can fix.


## Chapter 5: The productive years

**27** (`line-27.wav`, ~6s)
> From 15 to 49, the years of working, parenting and providing, the picture changes entirely.

**28** (`line-28.wav`, ~8s)
> HIV/AIDS is still the single largest killer of working-age Africans: 245,000 deaths in this band, three decades into the treatment era.

**29** (`line-29.wav`, ~7s)
> Tuberculosis: 146,000. Road injuries: 105,000. COVID-19: 98,000 in this age band in 2021 alone.

**30** (`line-30.wav`, ~5s)
> Interpersonal violence took 92,000 more. Self-harm, 49,000.

**31** (`line-31.wav`, ~10s)
> And about 151,000 women died of causes tied to pregnancy and childbirth. These are deaths the world has formally agreed should not happen. Four African countries currently meet the SDG maternal-mortality bar. Forty-three do not.


## Chapter 6: The NCD wave

**32** (`line-32.wav`, ~6s)
> Past fifty, the infections recede, and the chart fills with a different kind of death.

**33** (`line-33.wav`, ~7s)
> Ischaemic heart disease. Stroke. Hypertensive heart disease. Diabetes. The diseases the West spent fifty years learning to manage.

**34** (`line-34.wav`, ~7s)
> By 70 and older, heart disease and the two strokes together account for roughly a third of all deaths.

**35** (`line-35.wav`, ~6s)
> And COVID-19 holds a top-five place right through the oldest band: over 200,000 deaths among Africans over 70 in 2021.

**36** (`line-36.wav`, ~9s)
> One detail worth pausing on. African stroke is predominantly haemorrhagic, the bleeding kind, the signature of years of untreated high blood pressure. Almost the inverse of the Western pattern.


## Chapter 7: The double burden

**37** (`line-37.wav`, ~6s)
> Now line all five ages up together, each scaled to 100%.

**38** (`line-38.wav`, ~7s)
> The terracotta share, the diseases of poverty, falls with age. The violet share, the diseases of ageing, rises. They cross in the working years.

**39** (`line-39.wav`, ~11s)
> Africa is the only continent living both halves at once. We are dying twice. Once young, of things our great-grandparents died of. Once older, of things the rich world's grandparents died of.

**40** (`line-40.wav`, ~8s)
> Most health systems were built for one wave or the other. Africa's are being asked to handle both at the same time, on a fraction of the per-capita budget.


## Chapter 8: The cheap deaths

**41** (`line-41.wav`, ~7s)
> Come back to the children one last time. The interventions that would prevent most of these deaths are, by any standard, almost embarrassingly cheap.

**42** (`line-42.wav`, ~9s)
> A bednet: $3. A full course of childhood vaccines: $40. A packet of oral rehydration salts: 30 cents. A skilled birth attendant: about $50.

**43** (`line-43.wav`, ~8s)
> The NCD deaths further up the age range cost orders of magnitude more to prevent. But the cheapest health gains on Earth are still going unrealised, at scale, here.

**44** (`line-44.wav`, ~6s)
> The bottleneck is delivery, and underneath that, financing.
