# Voiceover recording script: 8.3 Million (v2, final)

One numbered line per audio clip. Save each take as `line-NN.wav`
(e.g. `line-01.wav`) into `apps/africa-causes-of-death-film/audio/`.

This is the v2 script agreed from `script-v2-review.xlsx`. The film's captions
already match this text, so record exactly what is written here.

Recording notes:
- Quiet room, soft furnishings, phone on silent, fridge/AC off if audible.
- Mouth 6-8 inches from the mic, speaking slightly past it, not into it.
- Record 2 seconds of room silence at the start of the session (for noise reduction), and re-record any line you fluff as a fresh take of just that line.
- Read at a calm, even documentary pace. The film will stretch to fit your timing, so do not rush.
- The caption durations below are the current on-screen timings, purely as a pace reference.


## Chapter 1: The outbreak

**01** (`line-01.wav`, ~5s)
> Two months ago, Uganda declared another Ebola outbreak.

**02** (`line-02.wav`, ~6s)
> One confirmed case was enough to set everything in motion: a press conference, an emergency command centre, and headlines around the world.

**03** (`line-03.wav`, ~5s)
> It has happened before, and it will happen again.

**04** (`line-04.wav`, ~7s)
> Here is a number that might surprise you. Since 1976, Ebola has killed about 15,000 people. That accounts for every outbreak, in every country, over almost fifty years.

**05** (`line-05.wav`, ~7s)
> Now let's look at malaria. In one bad week, malaria kills about the same number of people in Africa, and most of them are children.

**06** (`line-06.wav`, ~4s)
> That contrast got me thinking.

**07** (`line-07.wav`, ~7s)
> Emergencies like Ebola grab our attention, rightly so. However, the causes that quietly do most of the killing almost never make the news and are not as visible.


## Chapter 2: 8.3 million

**08** (`line-08.wav`, ~7s)
> In 2021, 8.3 million people died in the WHO African Region. That is every death, from every cause, at every age.

**09** (`line-09.wav`, ~6s)
> Every dot on this screen stands for 1,000 people. You are looking at all 8.3 million of them at once.

**10** (`line-10.wav`, ~5s)
> Health experts sort all these deaths into three big groups.

**11** (`line-11.wav`, ~16s)
> The first group is infections, plus deaths linked to pregnancy, childbirth and poor nutrition. Doctors call these communicable, maternal, perinatal and nutritional conditions. These are the diseases of poverty, and they took 4.6 million lives. That is more than half of the total.

**12** (`line-12.wav`, ~9s)
> The second group is non-communicable diseases, the ones you cannot catch from another person: heart disease, cancer, diabetes, stroke. They took 2.9 million lives.

**13** (`line-13.wav`, ~6s)
> The third group is injuries: road crashes, drowning, violence. They took 770,000 lives.

**14** (`line-14.wav`, ~8s)
> One cause does not fit neatly anywhere. The gold dots you see now are COVID-19. It killed 493,000 people here in 2021, its worst year on the continent.

**15** (`line-15.wav`, ~6s)
> But these three groups hide the question that matters most. How old were the people who died?

**16** (`line-16.wav`, ~5s)
> So let's sort the same 8.3 million dots by age instead.

**17** (`line-17.wav`, ~6s)
> 2.7 million of them were children under five. That is one out of every three deaths.


## Chapter 3: The first five years

**18** (`line-18.wav`, ~5s)
> For children under five, this chart is hard to look at.

**19** (`line-19.wav`, ~8s)
> Malaria killed 450,000 children. It is the single biggest killer of African children, and we have known how to prevent it for almost a hundred years.

**20** (`line-20.wav`, ~6s)
> Lung infections, mostly pneumonia, killed 427,000. Vaccines could have prevented most of these deaths.

**21** (`line-21.wav`, ~11s)
> Preterm birth took 381,000 babies. Diarrhoeal disease, which a thirty-cent packet of salts can treat, took 281,000. Birth asphyxia, when a baby cannot breathe during delivery, took 280,000.

**22** (`line-22.wav`, ~8s)
> Just five causes killed more than 1.8 million children in a single year. And the cheapest, best-proven interventions in public health are aimed at exactly this list.


## Chapter 4: The quiet years

**23** (`line-23.wav`, ~7s)
> Here is some good news. If a child makes it past five, the next ten years are the safest stretch of a human life.

**24** (`line-24.wav`, ~6s)
> Deaths drop to about 463,000. That is less than a fifth of the under-five number.

**25** (`line-25.wav`, ~7s)
> The causes change too. Infections are still on the list, but everyday accidents start climbing: road injuries and drowning.

**26** (`line-26.wav`, ~8s)
> Look at the second bar. After lung infections, the biggest single threat to a school-age child is a road accident. No doctor and no vaccine can fix that.


## Chapter 5: The productive years

**27** (`line-27.wav`, ~6s)
> From 15 to 49, the years of working and raising families, the picture changes completely.

**28** (`line-28.wav`, ~9s)
> HIV/AIDS is still the number one killer of working-age Africans. It took 245,000 lives in this age group, thirty years after good treatment became available.

**29** (`line-29.wav`, ~7s)
> Tuberculosis killed 146,000. Road injuries, 105,000. COVID-19, 98,000 in this age group in 2021 alone.

**30** (`line-30.wav`, ~5s)
> Violence took 92,000 more. Suicide, 49,000.

**31** (`line-31.wav`, ~12s)
> Still in this age group, about 151,000 women died from complications of pregnancy and childbirth. The world has agreed on a target for making these deaths rare. Today, four African countries meet it. Forty-three do not.


## Chapter 6: The NCD wave

**32** (`line-32.wav`, ~6s)
> Past fifty, the picture shifts again. A different kind of death starts filling the chart.

**33** (`line-33.wav`, ~16s)
> Look at the top of the chart. COVID-19 was the single biggest killer at this age in 2021. Right behind it come ischaemic heart disease and haemorrhagic stroke, the diseases rich countries spent fifty years learning to manage. And tuberculosis and HIV still have not let go.

**34** (`line-34.wav`, ~7s)
> By 70 and older, heart disease and the two kinds of stroke together cause more than one in four deaths.

**35** (`line-35.wav`, ~6s)
> And COVID-19 is still near the top here: over 200,000 deaths among Africans over 70 in 2021.

**36** (`line-36.wav`, ~14s)
> One detail is worth a pause. Most strokes in Africa are haemorrhagic, the bleeding kind. That is what years of untreated high blood pressure does to blood vessels. In rich countries it is mostly the opposite: the clotting kind, ischaemic stroke, is more common.


## Chapter 7: The double burden

**37** (`line-37.wav`, ~5s)
> Now put all five age groups side by side, each stretched to 100 percent.

**38** (`line-38.wav`, ~9s)
> The red share, the diseases of poverty, shrinks as people get older. The purple share, the diseases of ageing, grows. They trade places in the working years.

**39** (`line-39.wav`, ~10s)
> Africa is the only continent carrying both burdens at once. Children are still dying of the old diseases of poverty, and at the same time older adults are dying of the diseases of rich countries.

**40** (`line-40.wav`, ~8s)
> Most health systems were built to handle one of these burdens. African health systems are being asked to handle both at once, with far less money per person.


## Chapter 8: The cheap deaths

**41** (`line-41.wav`, ~7s)
> Let's come back to the children one last time. The interventions that would prevent most of these deaths are shockingly cheap.

**42** (`line-42.wav`, ~11s)
> A bednet costs three dollars. A full course of childhood vaccines, about forty dollars. A packet of oral rehydration salts, thirty cents. A trained birth attendant, about fifty dollars.

**43** (`line-43.wav`, ~9s)
> Preventing heart disease or cancer later in life costs far, far more. But these cheap wins for children are still not happening at full scale, right here where they are needed most.

**44** (`line-44.wav`, ~6s)
> The missing piece is getting these tools to every family, and finding the money to pay for it.
